Module 2 — Context-Aware Outfit Advisor

This project builds a context-aware outfit advisor. It reads local weather (temperature, humidity, wind, precipitation) and maps simple rules into daily clothing tips. Output combines a servo pointer gauge and LCD text. We start with fast cardboard prototypes and progress to a sensor + microcontroller build for real-world testing.

In-Class Activity 1 Geurrilla Prototyping I

Step 3: Experimentation — Outcomes

PromptOutcome
Describe the process of interpreting the video to design a template. I pulled an A-frame idea from the demo, measured my phone (width + case thickness), picked ~60° as the angle, sketched slot depth and rib positions, then cut cardboard.
Was it easy to jump right in or were you hesitant? Easy. I time-boxed 15 minutes for V1. Building fast revealed slot width and stability issues that shaped V2.
Did drawing help your creativity or design move forward easier? Yes. Quick pencil lines kept parts aligned, reduced recuts, and let me compare angles before cutting.
Was the change you made to the original design aesthetic or functional? Explain. Functional. V2 adds a rear wedge and a wider base, so it’s more stable, with better cable clearance and angle choices.

Step 4: Fabrication — Outcomes

PromptOutcome
Which material did you select and why? Thicker cardboard from a shipping box, because it provides better stability and support for the design.
Did your redesign make fabrication easier or more difficult? Easier. Fewer recuts and clearer joints. The rear wedge is a simple strip and quick to glue.
Describe how adhesive was or was not utilized. Hot-glue tacks on ribs for fast set; tape for temporary positioning; front slot left glue-less for shim tuning.
Was it easy or difficult collaborating on such a small-scale project? Easy. One measured/held while the other cut/glued. Quick decisions, little back-and-forth.
Could your design be improved using a laser-cutting solution? Yes. Cleaner edges, tab-and-slot joints. Increased precision and repeatability in cuts.

Activity 1 — DHT11 Sensor Exploration & Testing

Step 1 — Lists help

PromptKnownsUnknowns
Do you know which software you are using? Arduino IDE 2.x, Serial Monitor, Adafruit DHT library. Exact library version compatibility; preferred logging format for later dashboard use.
Do you know which sensor technology you need? DHT11 temperature/humidity module chosen for simplicity and availability. Accuracy and response time limits; need for calibration or averaging; enclosure airflow effects.
Do you know what your UI / UX looks like? Initial UI is text logs in Serial Monitor. Threshold indicators, error messages, update cadence, and possible web/LED feedback later.
Does your project use materials or will it be housed in a case of some type? Cardboard phone-stand prototype; small printed/plastic shell for the sensor. Cable routing and strain relief; venting so humidity readings are not trapped or delayed.

Step 2 — Identifying the Unknowns

UnknownsOutcome
Identify three UI related unknowns.
  • Units/precision to display
  • Status for bad reads (nan)
  • Update frequency labels
 Decide on °C with 1 decimal and %RH as integers; print explicit error lines; show timestamped updates.
Identify three UX related unknowns.
  • Readability at a glance
  • Feedback when values go out of range
  • Placement that avoids hand-heat bias
 Larger font/concise lines; add threshold notices; mount sensor away from hands/warm components.
Identify three Hardware or Software related unknowns.
  • Correct pin and pull-up needs
  • Stable power via USB vs external
  • Minimum safe sampling period
 Use module’s onboard pull-up; keep USB power; set polling ≥1 s per DHT11 spec to reduce read errors.
Identify three Interaction related unknowns.
  • Thresholds that trigger feedback
  • Mapping to stand form (light/alert)
  • Noise smoothing vs responsiveness
 Prototype LED cue for hot/dry; plan a moving average over 5 samples; keep latency under ~5 s.

Step 3 — Strategizing

Identified UnknownsOutcome
Identify two things you need that are slowing you down or stopping your progress. Unstable reads when polling too fast; mechanical mounting/strain relief. Action: slow to ≥1 s, reseat connectors, add tape/zip-tie relief.
List the things you are most-curious about, put them in order of interest. 1) Humidity lag vs breath test 2) Enclosure vents effect 3) Self-heating near MCU. Action: quick A/B tests with open vs closed vents and board distance.
Identify two blind spots or obstacles about your project that need to be overcome to move forward. Power noise from USB hubs; cable stress breaking headers. Mitigation: use direct USB port and gentle cable path.

Step 4 — Sensor Testing: Outcomes

PromptOutcome
What single action did you test? End-to-end DHT11 read with Serial logging to verify sensor → code → monitor.
Board, library, and wiring Arduino with Adafruit DHT (DHT.h); module 5V and GND; DATA to D2; module’s pull-up used.
Sampling and baud 115200 baud; polling every 1–2 s to match DHT11’s ~1 Hz capability.
Validation method Serial Monitor shows stable values; warm breath raises %RH and slightly raises °C; baseline matches room meter.
Issues and fixes Occasional nan when too fast or with loose jumper; fixed by slower polling and reseating connectors.
